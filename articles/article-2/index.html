8 Steps for Building and Evaluating ML Models

July 28, 2021

In this article we will briefly describe the steps to follow to build a good machine learning model. Although probably the most exciting part of the ML world is the development and testing of models, these will be of little or no use if a series of steps have not been carried out beforehand to evaluate and guarantee the quality of the information we are working with, or to check that we are really meeting the needs of the business. On the other hand, every model has a certain useful life, since the context and conditions in which it is developed may change constantly. For this reason, once a model has been implemented, it is necessary to constantly work on its adaptation and optimization.

Although different sources group or divide the steps to follow according to convenience, they all agree on the most important.

Step N°1: Understand the business problem (and define success)

You need to know what problem you’re trying to solve before attempting to solve it. Make sure you understand the project’s objectives and requirements, and convert this knowledge into a suitable problem definition for the machine learning project. Then, make a plan for achieving the project’s objectives.

Keep in mind that not all problems are necessarily problems to be solved by machine learning. ML models are just one more - very powerful - tool to be taken into account.

Some questions you may want to answer are:

  • What’s the business objective that requires a cognitive solution?
  • What are the defined “success” criteria for the project?
  • What parts of the solution are cognitive, and what aren’t?
  • Have all the necessary technical, business and deployment issues been addressed?
  • What are the characteristics of the problem being solved? Is this a classification, regression or clustering problem?
  • How can the project be staged in iterative sprints?
  • Are there any special requirements for transparency, explainability or bias reduction?
  • What are the ethical considerations?
  • What are the acceptable parameters for accuracy, precision and confusion matrix values?
  • What are the expected inputs to the model and the expected outputs?
  • How will the benefits of the model be measured?

Step N°2: Understand and identify data

A machine learning model is built by learning and generalizing from training data, then applying that acquired knowledge to new data it has never seen before to make predictions and fulfill its purpose. Lack of data will prevent you from building the model, and access to data isn’t enough. Useful data needs to be clean and in a good shape.

Identify your data needs and determine whether the data is in proper shape for the machine learning project. You will need to consider:

  • What data resources are needed and if you have access to them
  • How much data will you need
  • Determine the current quality and quantity of the data
  • How will you split the data into train and test set
  • Is the data labeled? WIll you need to label the data?
  • Are there any pre-trained models that you can use?

Step N°3: Collect the data

The quality and quantity of information you get are very important since it will directly impact how well or badly your model will work. You may have the information in an existing database or you must create it from scratch. If it is a small project you can create a spreadsheet that will later be easily exported as a CSV file. It is also common to use the web scraping technique to automatically collect information from various sources such as APIs.

Step N°4: Prepare the data

Data preparation and cleansing tasks can take a substantial amount of time. Surveys of machine learning developers and data scientists show that the data collection and preparation steps can take up to 80% of a machine learning project’s time. As the saying goes, “garbage in, garbage out.” Since machine learning models need to learn from data, the amount of time spent on prepping and cleansing is well worth it.

Some things you have to do in this step are:

  • Visualize your data and check if there are correlations between the different characteristics
  • Make a selection of characteristics to use in the model, since the ones you choose will directly impact the execution times and the results
  • Balance the amount of data we have for each result or class, to prevent the model to be biased towards any type of response
  • Separate the data into train and test.
  • Normalizing
  • Handle missing values
  • Handle duplicates and outliers

Step N°5: Choose the model and train it

There are several models that you can choose according to the objective that you might have: you will use algorithms of classification, prediction, linear regression, clustering, i.e. k-means or K-Nearest Neighbor, Deep Learning, i.e Neural Networks, Bayesian, etc. The model selection also depends on the data you are going to process such as images, sound, text, and numerical values.

This phase requires model technique selection and application, model training, model hyperparameter setting and adjustment, model validation, ensemble model development and testing, algorithm selection, and model optimization.

The resulting model can then be evaluated to determine whether it meets the business and operational requirements.

Step N°6: Evaluate the model’s performance

You have to evaluate the models using a validation data set. From an AI perspective, evaluation includes model metric evaluation, confusion matrix calculations, KPIs, model performance metrics, model quality measurements and a final determination of whether the model can meet the established business goals.

It is also important to compare the machine learning model to the baseline model or heuristic, to see if we reached a superior solution.

If you don’t reached the results you expected (or at least an acceptable result), you may have to iterate over steps 4 to 6, or even try to collect more data.

Step N°7: Put the model in production and make sure it works well

When you’re confident that the machine learning model can work in the real world, it’s time to see how it actually operates – also known as “operationalizing” the model:

  • Deploy the model with a means to continually measure and monitor its performance.
  • Develop a baseline or benchmark against which future iterations of the model can be measured.
  • Continuously iterate on different aspects of the model to improve overall performance.

Model operationalization might include deployment scenarios in a cloud environment, at the edge, in an on-premises or closed environment, or within a closed, controlled group. Among operationalization considerations are model versioning and iteration, model deployment, model monitoring and model staging in development and production environments. Depending on the requirements, model operationalization can range from simply generating a report to a more complex, multi-endpoint deployment.

Step N° 8: Iterate and adjust the model

Even though the model is operational and you’re continuously monitoring its performance, you’re not done. Business requirements, technology capabilities and real-world data changes in unexpected ways. All of which might create new requirements for deploying the model onto different endpoints or in new systems.
